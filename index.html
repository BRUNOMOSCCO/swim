Basic facts about swimming

Swimming is a progressive movement of a living body in a water environment.
For human swimming the earliest documentation of human swimming is in stone age paintings about 7,000 years ago

In 1800 competitive swimming competitions began to be held in Europe, most of the competitions were held breaststroke style.
Swimming was already one of the Olympic disciplines in the first modern Olympics in 1896 in Athens.

Unlike sports such as running or riding, swimming activates more muscle groups in the body.
The main muscles in swimming: triceps, back, abdominal muscles and legs.

In addition, swimming Helps those suffering from breathing problems and asthmatics to strengthen the lungs and increase their resistance.
Swimming makes it possible to improve and maintain aerobic and anaerobic physical fitness and helps maintain the blood, heart and lung systems.

The four main swimming styles

Front crawl
In front crawl the swimmer sweeps the water with both hands
Alternately so that one hand always scoops up the water, and moves the legs, each separately, up and down.
Breathing is done to the sides (there is no obligation to breathe any fixed number of movements).

Breaststroke
The most difficult swimming style Technically it is the slowest.
In popular swimming, breaststroke is considered a fairly easy style, because it is comfortable and effective when the goal is not fast progress.
In breaststroke, the swimmer makes a circular movement with his hands - the hands are sent forward
when they Stretched, the swimmer scoops up water while bringing his hands closer to his chest and stretches them again.
The swimmer's legs fold and then forcefully push back to push the water.
Breathing is done by taking the head out of the water once in each raking and kicking cycle.

Backstroke
The backstroker has the advantage of not having to take his head out of the water.
To breathe while swimming, but on the other hand, there is the disadvantage of difficulty seeing direction the progress.
It is also the only competitive swimming style where the jump is made from the water.
In the backstroke, the swimmer straightens his hands and sweeps them alternately so that one hand is always sweeping water.
Backstroke legs alternately rise and fall similar to rowing. Backstroke is the second slowest style, after breaststroke.

Butterfly
The butterfly swimmer rakes the water with both hands at the same time, while raking the water
the rest of his body makes a "dolphin" movement which raises him up so he can breathe.
During the rake cycle of the hands, the legs perform two "kicks" with close legs - one "kick" before the
rake with the hands extended forward and one after the rake with the hands extended backward.

My knowledge transfer methods

As a swimming instructor the most important thing is to listen.
It is important for me to understand the student's need, and throughout the learning process to listen to the student's feelings.
I first want to identify the desire and need of my student, and then slowly in personalized steps, I connect the student with his water personality.

The process has no time limit and everything depends on the student in front of me and his level of closeness to the water.
The most important message I have, after several years as a swimming instructor is - everyone can swim and stay in the water, and even enjoy it.

In the first meeting, I know a beginner in his learning process, I ask general background questions and teach the basic rules for starting school.

In the following sessions, the student's progress is slowly tested.
The continuation of studies depends on the student's desire to deepen in the water and in the field of swimming
